Cognitive Benefits

  • Spatial Reasoning — Trains your ability to mentally manipulate 2D shapes, evaluate spatial fit, and plan placements
  • Mental Rotation — Strengthens the capacity to rotate and flip shapes in your mind before placing them
  • Combinatorial Planning — Develops systematic search strategies for fitting multiple pieces into a constrained space
  • Visual Pattern Recognition — Sharpens your ability to recognize how shapes interlock and complement each other
  • Executive Function — Exercises planning ahead, backtracking from mistakes, and adapting strategies
  • Visuospatial Working Memory — Keeps multiple piece shapes and their possible orientations active in working memory simultaneously

How It Works

You are given a set of polyomino pieces (shapes made of connected squares) and a board. The board shape is formed by assembling the pieces together — every cell must be filled, no gaps, no overlaps. Your job is to figure out where each piece goes.

Controls

Drag & Drop: Drag a piece from the tray onto the board. A ghost preview shows where it will land — green for valid, red for invalid. Release to place.

Click to Select: Tap a piece in the tray to select it, then tap a board cell to place it there.

Board Pieces: Click a placed piece to select it (highlighted). Double-click to remove it back to the tray. Drag a placed piece to move it to a new position.

Rotate & Flip

Use the ↺ ↻ buttons to rotate the selected piece left or right, and ⇔ to flip it horizontally. Works on both tray pieces and pieces already on the board. If an in-place rotation doesn't fit, the buttons flash red.

Keyboard: R to rotate right, F to flip.

Hints

Tap 💡 for a hint. If any piece is in the wrong position, the hint removes it. If all placed pieces are correct, the hint places the next piece at its solution position. Some puzzles may have multiple valid solutions — hints guide toward one specific solution, but any arrangement that fills the board completely counts as a win.

Difficulty

The difficulty slider controls which pieces are available. Lower difficulty uses simpler, more symmetric shapes (like the square or I-bar). Higher difficulty introduces complex asymmetric pieces with many possible orientations — harder to mentally rotate and fit.

The piece count slider controls how many pieces you need to place. More pieces = larger board = more combinations to consider.
